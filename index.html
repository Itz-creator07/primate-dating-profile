🌿 About Me

👋 Hello!

I am Kipunji — one of the rarest monkeys in the world. First spotted in 2003 and formally described as a new species in 2006, I belong to Rungwecebus kipunji — the first new monkey genus named since 1923. My genus name means "slender monkey from Rungwe." If you're looking for someone truly one of a kind… you just found them.

✨ My Look

  • Distinctive mohawk-like crest and long cheek whiskers that point downward
  • Fur ranges from light gray to brown, with a pale patch on the chest
  • Dark face, prominent muzzle, black eyelids
  • Body: 33.5–33.7 inches (85–90 cm); weight 22–35 lbs (10–16 kg)
  • Tail longer than half my body, sometimes tipped with pale or white fur
  • Males develop a mantle of long, bushy shoulder fur

📍 Where I'm From

Country: Tanzania, East Africa

Two isolated populations:

  • Rungwe-Livingstone forest (SW Tanzania) — ~90% of all kipunjis
  • Ndundulu Forest, Udzungwa Mountains

Elevation: 4,265–8,038 ft (1,300–2,450 m) above sea level — high, misty mountain forests.

⚠️ Why I Need You

I am Endangered. Only two isolated populations exist, separated by 217.5 miles of deforested land. The Ndundulu group may already be below a viable reproductive threshold.

This isn't just a connection — it's part of keeping a species alive.

The good news? A 2021 census found 1,966 kipunjis — a 65% increase since 2006, thanks to habitat protection. Every match matters for Rungwecebus kipunji.

🌈 Who I'm Open To

Like most Kipunji, my observed behavior follows heterosexual patterns — I am a male seeking a female partner for long-term bonding and raising offspring together within our social group.

That said, within our multi-male, multi-female troop, social bonds come in many forms. Same-sex affiliative behaviors — grooming, proximity, alliance-building — are part of healthy troop life and contribute to group stability and trust.

What matters most to me is connection, cooperation, and survival — together.

🐾 Typical Behaviors

🌳

Almost Entirely Arboreal

I spend almost all my time in the middle and upper forest canopy — sleeping above 65 feet (20 m). I move quickly, leaping long distances branch to branch. I rarely touch the ground.

🔊

Four Distinct Calls

I have a vocabulary: a low "honk-bark" to space out groups, "chutters" while foraging, "squeals" for long-distance family contact, and a high-pitched chirp as an eagle alarm call.

☀️

Diurnal & Active

I'm most active from 8–11:30 a.m. and 3–6 p.m., foraging most of the day and covering about 0.8 miles (1.3 km) daily. I rest during the hottest midday hours.

🤝

Social Grooming

Grooming maintains bonds, social rank, and trust within the group. I share sleeping sites with Moloney's white-collared monkeys and Udzungwa red colobus — peaceful co-existence is my style.

🥶

Cold-Weather Survivor

My mountain forests can drop below 32°F (0°C). My thick fur keeps me warm, and my group huddles together to sleep on cold nights. We look out for each other.

👥

Group Living

Groups of 12 to 36 individuals — multiple females, their young, and several competing males. We are not territorial, generally avoid conflict, and flee into the canopy when startled.

🌍 Ecology & Diet

🏔️ Habitat

Type: Montane mixed forests, cool high-altitude ecosystems

Elevation: 4,265–8,038 ft (1,300–2,450 m)

Locations:

  • Rungwe-Livingstone forest, SW Tanzania (~42 km²)
  • Ndundulu Forest, Udzungwa Mountains (~25 km²)

Temperatures can drop below freezing. Deforestation, illegal logging, and mining are my greatest threats.

🌅 Imagine Us Together

☀️ Our Perfect First Date

Picture this: early morning in the Udzungwa Mountains. The mist is still clinging to the canopy as I find you foraging in the fig trees — your fur catching the first light of dawn. I approach slowly, calmly. No rush. That's how you know I'm confident without being careless.

We spend the morning moving through the treetops together, side by side, picking the ripest fruits 🍇 and sharing the best branches. I show you my favorite lookout point — a high fork in an old tree where you can see across the whole valley. No predators. Just us and the forest below.

By midday, we settle into a quiet patch of canopy. I groom your crest — yes, that signature messy mohawk 😄 — and you let me. That's when I know we might just work. A good first date in our world is simple: safety, food, and trust.

🌿 Growing Old Together

I see us as core members of our troop — the ones the younger ones look to when something feels wrong, or when a new fruit tree is found. We move through the forest like we were made for it — because we were.

Together we raise our offspring in the safety of the group, teaching them the vocal calls that keep us connected, the balance needed to leap between branches, and the social awareness that keeps the whole troop strong.

As years pass — and in the wild we may reach up to 45 of them — I want a partner who still shares a branch with me at sunset, watching over the valley we've protected together. Not dramatic. Not loud. Just present, aware, and alive — high above the noise. 🌄

"Rare, aware, and high above the noise — if you can keep up in the trees, we might just grow old up here together."
